Title: Ultrasound-Based Quantitative Assessment of Pleural Effusion

Radiological Findings:
- Thoracic Ultrasound (TUS):
  - **Anechoic (dark) fluid** → typically transudative
  - **Echogenic fluid** with internal echoes → suggests exudative effusion
  - **Septations and fibrin strands** → complicated effusion/empyema
  - **Floating debris** → inflammatory or infectious process
- Quantitative features:
  - Increased **pixel density / echogenicity** in exudates
  - Hypoechogenicity index variation between fluid types
- CT correlation:
  - Pleural thickening or enhancement (exudative/malignant)
  - Associated lung pathology

Causes:
- Transudative:
  - Heart failure
- Exudative:
  - Infection (parapneumonic, empyema)
  - Malignancy
  - Inflammatory conditions

Symptoms:
- Dyspnea
- Chest discomfort
- Fever (in infectious cases)

Diagnosis:
- Ultrasound for real-time detection and characterization
- Quantitative image analysis (pixel density, echogenicity metrics)
- Integration with:
  - Clinical findings
  - Laboratory data (fluid analysis when available)
- AI-assisted models (emerging):
  - Combine imaging + clinical + lab data for prediction
- Thoracentesis remains gold standard for confirmation

Clinical Importance:
- Provides **noninvasive characterization** of pleural effusion
- Helps differentiate transudate vs exudate when fluid sampling is limited
- Useful in bedside and critical care settings
- AI integration may improve standardization and diagnostic accuracy
- Currently investigational but promising for future clinical workflows

Differential Diagnosis:
- Simple transudative effusion (anechoic, no septations)
- Complicated parapneumonic effusion/empyema (septations, debris)
- Malignant pleural effusion (echogenic with pleural abnormalities)
- Hemothorax (echogenic due to blood content)
