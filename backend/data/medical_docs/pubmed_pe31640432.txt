Title: Diagnostic Approach to Undiagnosed Pleural Effusion

Radiological Findings:
- Blunting of costophrenic angle
- Homogeneous lower zone opacity with meniscus sign
- Variable size: small to massive effusion
- Ultrasound:
  - Anechoic or complex fluid
  - Septations in empyema
- CT findings:
  - Pleural thickening or nodularity (suggests malignancy)
  - Associated lung pathology (infection, mass, embolism)
  - Fluid characterization and extent

Causes:
- Common:
  - Heart failure (transudative)
  - Infection (parapneumonic, tuberculosis)
  - Malignancy
- Others:
  - Pulmonary embolism
  - Systemic diseases
  - Chylothorax
- Unknown etiology in some cases

Symptoms:
- Dyspnea
- Chest discomfort
- Cough

Diagnosis:
- Chest X-ray for initial detection
- Ultrasound for guidance and characterization
- CT for detailed evaluation of pleura and lung
- Thoracentesis (image-guided):
  - Fluid analysis (transudate vs exudate)
  - Microbiology and cytology
- Pleural biopsy if diagnosis remains unclear
- Advanced tests:
  - Nucleic acid amplification tests
  - Pleural fluid cultures (enhanced techniques)

Clinical Importance:
- Accurate diagnosis requires integration of imaging + clinical + lab data
- Image-guided procedures improve diagnostic yield and safety
- Early identification of malignant or infectious causes is critical
- Follow-up important in initially undiagnosed cases

Differential Diagnosis:
- Malignant pleural effusion
- Tuberculous pleural effusion
- Parapneumonic effusion/empyema
- Congestive heart failure-related effusion
- Chylothorax
