Title: Radiological Classification and Imaging Features of Pneumonia

Radiological Findings:
- Lobar pneumonia:
  - Dense, homogeneous consolidation involving a single lobe
  - Air bronchograms commonly present
- Bronchopneumonia:
  - Patchy, multifocal consolidations
  - Peribronchial distribution with scattered opacities
- Interstitial pneumonia:
  - Diffuse reticular or reticulonodular pattern
  - Ground-glass opacities
  - Bilateral, symmetric involvement (common in atypical/viral causes)
- CT findings:
  - Detects early or subtle consolidation not seen on X-ray
  - Better visualization of ground-glass opacities and interstitial involvement
- Complications:
  - Pleural effusion (blunting of costophrenic angle)
  - Empyema (loculated pleural fluid)
  - Lung abscess (cavity with air-fluid level)

Causes:
- Bacterial (e.g., Streptococcus pneumoniae → lobar pattern)
- Viral infections → interstitial pattern
- Atypical organisms (e.g., Mycoplasma pneumoniae)
- Hospital-acquired pathogens → bronchopneumonia

Symptoms:
- Cough
- Fever
- Shortness of breath

Diagnosis:
- Primary: Chest X-ray for pattern recognition
- CT scan for unclear or severe cases
- Ultrasound for pleural effusion detection and drainage guidance
- Clinical correlation with infection signs

Clinical Importance:
- Radiological pattern helps classify pneumonia type → guides treatment
- Imaging essential for detecting complications (effusion, abscess)
- CT improves diagnostic accuracy in complex or hospital cases
- Ultrasound assists in safe management of pleural complications

Differential Diagnosis:
- Pulmonary edema (bilateral opacities, different distribution)
- Tuberculosis (cavitary lesions, upper lobe predominance)
- Lung malignancy (mass-like consolidation)
- ARDS (diffuse bilateral ground-glass opacities)
