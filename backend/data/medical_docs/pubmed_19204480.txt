Title: High-Attenuation Pulmonary Consolidations on CT: Differential Diagnosis

Radiological Findings:
- Key feature: Consolidation with **high attenuation (hyperdense areas)** on CT
- Patterns:
  - Diffuse or focal **hyperdense air-space consolidation**
  - Dense opacities higher than soft tissue (suggestive of calcification or deposition)
- Distribution clues:
  - Diffuse bilateral involvement (e.g., metabolic or deposition diseases)
  - Focal or segmental consolidation (e.g., infection, embolism)
- Disease-specific patterns:
  - Metastatic pulmonary calcification → diffuse, upper lobe predominant calcified opacities
  - Pulmonary alveolar microlithiasis → diffuse sand-like calcified micronodules
  - Amiodarone lung → high-attenuation consolidation due to iodine content
  - Talcosis → dense nodules/conglomerates, often upper lobes
  - Iodinated oil embolism → hyperdense embolic material in vessels/alveoli
  - Tuberculosis → consolidation with possible calcifications
  - Silicoproteinosis → ground-glass + dense opacities
  - Amyloidosis → focal or diffuse dense deposits

Causes:
- Calcium deposition (metastatic calcification)
- Inhaled or deposited materials (talc, silica)
- Drug-induced (amiodarone)
- Protein or amyloid deposition
- Infectious causes (e.g., tuberculosis)
- Embolic substances (iodinated oil)

Symptoms:
- Cough
- Dyspnea
- May be asymptomatic in early deposition diseases

Diagnosis:
- CT scan identifying **hyperdense consolidation pattern**
- Pattern recognition based on **attenuation + distribution**
- Clinical history (drug use, occupational exposure, infection risk)
- Additional lab or microbiological tests if infection suspected

Clinical Importance:
- High-attenuation consolidation is a **key diagnostic clue** for non-typical lung diseases
- Helps differentiate **deposition, metabolic, drug-induced, and infectious causes**
- Reduces diagnostic ambiguity compared to standard consolidation
- Guides targeted investigations and management

Differential Diagnosis:
- Typical pneumonia (non-calcified consolidation)
- Pulmonary hemorrhage (can mimic consolidation but lacks high attenuation pattern)
- Neoplastic lesions with calcification
- Chronic granulomatous diseases
