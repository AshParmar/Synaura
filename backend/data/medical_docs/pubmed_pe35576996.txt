Title: Malignant Pleural Effusion on Imaging in Advanced Cancer

Radiological Findings:
- Large **unilateral pleural effusion** (most common)
- Homogeneous opacity with **meniscus sign**
- Possible **mediastinal shift** (if massive)
- CT findings:
  - **Pleural thickening and nodularity** (key malignant feature)
  - Irregular pleural enhancement
  - Associated lung mass or metastases
- Ultrasound:
  - Complex fluid with septations
  - Pleural or diaphragmatic nodules

Causes:
- Metastatic spread to pleura
- Most commonly from:
  - Lung cancer
  - Breast cancer
- Advanced-stage malignancy

Symptoms:
- Dyspnea (most common)
- Chest discomfort
- Reduced exercise tolerance

Diagnosis:
- Chest X-ray detecting pleural effusion
- CT scan (gold standard) for pleural abnormalities
- Ultrasound for characterization and guidance
- Pleural fluid cytology (via thoracentesis)
- Pleural biopsy if cytology inconclusive

Clinical Importance:
- Indicates **advanced malignancy (stage progression)**
- Associated with poor prognosis
- Imaging features (nodularity, thickening) suggest malignancy
- Management is mainly **palliative** (symptom relief)
- Guides interventions like drainage or pleurodesis

Differential Diagnosis:
- Parapneumonic effusion (infection-related)
- Tuberculous pleural effusion
- Congestive heart failure (transudative, usually bilateral)
- Empyema (loculated infected fluid)
