Title: Radiological Patterns of Bacterial Pneumonia

Bacterial pneumonia is a common lung infection characterized by inflammation of lung tissue and accumulation of fluid or pus in the alveolar spaces. Imaging plays a crucial role in its detection, characterization, and follow-up.

Radiological Findings:
On chest X-ray, bacterial pneumonia typically presents as air-space consolidation. The most common pattern is lobar consolidation, where a single lobe or segment is affected. Other patterns include bronchopneumonia with patchy opacities and diffuse involvement in severe cases.

Specific radiological patterns include:
- Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP): localized lobar or segmental consolidation
- Aspiration pneumonia: bilateral, lower lobe predominant opacities
- Nosocomial pneumonia: diffuse, multifocal opacities often associated with pleural effusion

Causes:
Bacterial pneumonia is caused by bacterial infections and is classified into community-acquired, aspiration-related, and hospital-acquired (nosocomial) forms depending on the clinical setting.

Diagnosis:
Chest radiography is the primary imaging modality due to its availability and effectiveness. CT imaging is used in complicated or unresolved cases to better visualize disease extent and associated complications.

Clinical Importance:
Early and accurate detection is essential, particularly in nosocomial pneumonia, which is associated with high mortality. Imaging also plays a key role in monitoring disease progression and identifying complications such as abscess or pleural effusion.

Differential Diagnosis:
Bacterial pneumonia may resemble viral pneumonia (e.g., COVID-19), pulmonary edema, or acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), and should be differentiated based on imaging patterns and clinical context.
