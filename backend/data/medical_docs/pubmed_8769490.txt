Title: Quantitative CT-Based Detection of Early Pulmonary Edema

Pulmonary edema can be quantitatively assessed using computed tomography (CT) to detect early increases in lung water content, particularly in patients with congestive heart failure.

Radiological Findings:
On chest X-ray, pulmonary edema may appear as bilateral opacities and vascular congestion; however, early stages may not be clearly visible. On CT imaging, pulmonary edema is associated with increased lung density, reflected by changes in Hounsfield units (HU), particularly in the range of -650 to -750 HU.

Causes:
Pulmonary edema in this context is primarily associated with left-sided heart failure and elevated pulmonary wedge pressure, leading to fluid accumulation in lung tissues.

Diagnosis:
CT imaging enables early detection of pulmonary edema by measuring changes in lung density. A quantitative metric, such as the percentage of pixels (%PXL) within a specific Hounsfield unit range, correlates with pulmonary wedge pressure and indicates increased lung water content, even before abnormalities are visible on chest radiographs.

Clinical Importance:
Quantitative CT analysis provides a sensitive method for detecting subclinical pulmonary edema and monitoring disease progression. This allows earlier intervention and better management of patients with heart failure.

Differential Diagnosis:
Pulmonary edema detected on CT may resemble interstitial lung disease or pneumonia and should be differentiated using clinical and imaging context.
