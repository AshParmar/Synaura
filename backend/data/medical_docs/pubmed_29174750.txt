Title: Pulmonary Edema Diagnosis and Radiological Features

Pulmonary edema is a condition characterized by the accumulation of fluid in the lung interstitium and alveolar spaces. It is increasingly recognized as a critical complication affecting patient outcomes, particularly in perioperative and intensive care settings.

Radiological Findings:
On chest X-ray, pulmonary edema typically presents as bilateral diffuse opacities, often described as a "bat-wing" pattern. Additional features may include Kerley B lines, vascular congestion, and fluid accumulation in the lower lung zones.

Causes:
Pulmonary edema may be cardiogenic, caused by heart failure or excessive fluid administration, or non-cardiogenic, resulting from increased pulmonary capillary permeability due to inflammatory processes.

Diagnosis:
Early diagnosis is essential and can be achieved through imaging techniques such as chest radiography, lung ultrasonography, and advanced methods like transpulmonary thermodilution. Chest X-ray remains a primary tool for identifying fluid accumulation and monitoring disease progression.

Clinical Importance:
Timely detection and differentiation between hydrostatic and permeability-related edema are crucial for appropriate treatment. Accurate assessment of extravascular lung water helps guide therapy and improve patient outcomes.

Differential Diagnosis:
Pulmonary edema may resemble pneumonia, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), or pleural effusion on imaging and should be carefully distinguished.
