Title: Pleural Fluid Analysis in Differentiating Types of Pleural Effusion

Radiological Findings:
- Chest X-ray:
  - Blunting of costophrenic angle
  - Homogeneous basal opacity with meniscus sign
- Ultrasound:
  - Simple (anechoic) or complex (septated) effusion
- CT findings:
  - Pleural thickening or nodularity (malignancy)
  - Associated consolidation (parapneumonic effusion)
  - Lymphadenopathy (tuberculosis or malignancy)

Causes:
- Transudative:
  - Heart failure
- Exudative:
  - Parapneumonic infection
  - Tuberculosis
  - Malignancy (including mesothelioma)
- Others:
  - Systemic diseases

Symptoms:
- Dyspnea
- Chest pain
- Fever (infection-related cases)

Diagnosis:
- Imaging (X-ray, CT, ultrasound) for detection and characterization
- Thoracentesis with pleural fluid analysis:
  - Light’s criteria → transudate vs exudate
  - Elevated NT-proBNP → suggests heart failure
  - Neutrophil predominance + high CRP → parapneumonic effusion
  - Low pH (<7.2) → complicated effusion/empyema
  - Lymphocyte predominance + high ADA → tuberculosis
  - Tumor markers (mesothelin, fibulin-3) → mesothelioma
- Cytology and immunohistochemistry for malignancy differentiation

Clinical Importance:
- Pleural fluid analysis is **central to diagnosis**
- Enables identification of underlying mechanism and cause
- Guides treatment decisions (e.g., antibiotics, drainage, oncology care)
- Prevents high rates of undiagnosed effusions

Differential Diagnosis:
- Malignant pleural effusion
- Tuberculous pleural effusion
- Parapneumonic effusion/empyema
- Heart failure-related effusion
- Mesothelioma
