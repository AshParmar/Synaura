Title: Pleural Effusion on Chest Imaging: Patterns and Clinical Differentiation

Radiological Findings:
- Blunting of **costophrenic angle** (early sign)
- Homogeneous opacity in lower lung zones
- **Meniscus sign** (curved upper fluid level)
- Large effusion:
  - Complete or near-complete hemithorax opacification
  - Mediastinal shift (if massive)
- Layering fluid on lateral decubitus view
- Ultrasound:
  - Anechoic or complex fluid collection
  - Septations in complicated effusion/empyema
- CT findings:
  - Fluid density in pleural space
  - Pleural thickening or enhancement (suggests exudate/empyema)

Causes:
- Transudative:
  - Congestive heart failure (most common)
- Exudative:
  - Pneumonia (parapneumonic effusion)
  - Malignancy (lung, breast cancer)
  - Pulmonary embolism
- Complicated:
  - Empyema (infected pleural fluid)

Symptoms:
- Dyspnea
- Chest pain (pleuritic)
- Cough

Diagnosis:
- Chest X-ray detecting pleural fluid
- Ultrasound for confirmation and characterization
- CT for detailed evaluation and underlying cause
- Pleural tap (thoracentesis):
  - Differentiates **transudate vs exudate**
  - Guides further diagnosis

Clinical Importance:
- Very common finding with wide range of causes
- Imaging helps assess size, complexity, and complications
- Differentiation of transudate vs exudate is critical for management
- Early detection of empyema prevents severe complications
- Guides interventions (drainage, pleurodesis, catheter placement)

Differential Diagnosis:
- Lung consolidation (no meniscus sign)
- Atelectasis (volume loss present)
- Pleural thickening (no free fluid layering)
- Hemothorax or chylothorax (based on fluid characteristics)
