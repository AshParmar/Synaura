Title: Radiological Signs and Types of Atelectasis on Chest X-ray

Radiological Findings:
- Key feature: **volume loss**
- Direct signs:
  - **Crowding of pulmonary vessels**
  - Crowded or increased visibility of air bronchograms
  - **Displacement of interlobar fissures**
- Indirect signs:
  - Increased lung opacity (may be subtle or absent)
  - **Mediastinal shift toward affected side**
  - Elevation of hemidiaphragm
  - Displacement of hilum
  - Rib crowding (narrowed intercostal spaces)
  - Compensatory hyperinflation of adjacent lung
- Patterns:
  - Segmental or lobar collapse
  - Linear/discoid (plate-like) atelectasis
  - Diffuse or whole lung collapse

Causes:
- Airway obstruction (resorption atelectasis)
- Surfactant deficiency (adhesive atelectasis)
- External compression (pleural effusion, mass, pneumothorax)
- Fibrosis (cicatrization atelectasis)
- Hypoventilation or gravity-dependent changes

Symptoms:
- Dyspnea
- Reduced breath sounds
- May be asymptomatic in small areas

Diagnosis:
- Chest X-ray identifying **volume loss + structural displacement**
- CT scan to identify underlying cause (e.g., obstruction)
- Clinical correlation essential
- Microbiological evidence required if infection suspected (atelectatic pneumonia)

Clinical Importance:
- Very common but often **misdiagnosed as pneumonia**
- Recognition of volume loss is key differentiator
- Helps identify underlying causes (obstruction, compression, fibrosis)
- Guides appropriate management (physiotherapy, bronchoscopy, etc.)

Differential Diagnosis:
- Pneumonia (opacity without volume loss)
- Pleural effusion (mass effect, shifts away)
- Lung mass (no volume loss, focal lesion)
- Pulmonary fibrosis (chronic structural distortion)
